## The talent myth

When I started learning programming in 2023, I believed people who coded well were born with some special ability. That there was a developer gene.

Two years later, I can say with certainty: that's a lie.

What exists is **consistency**. And consistency is a choice you make every day.

## What I did differently

In 2024, I worked during the day and studied at night. I didn't have even two years of coding experience. But I had one thing many didn't: I **showed up every day**.

It didn't matter if I was tired. It didn't matter if the lesson was boring. I opened the computer, wrote code, made mistakes, fixed them, and slept. Repeated the next day.

> "Success doesn't come from doing extraordinary things, but from doing ordinary things with extraordinary consistency."

## The compounding of learning

There's a concept in finance called compound interest. The idea is simple: small gains accumulated over time generate exponential results.

Learning works the same way.

Every day you study, you don't just learn the content of that day. You build on everything that came before. Connections form. What seemed hard last week feels obvious today.

## Applying in practice

Three principles that worked for me:

1. **Minimum daily progress**: write at least 30 lines of code per day, no matter what.
2. **Real projects**: learning from a tutorial is different from building something people actually use.
3. **Patience with the process**: results don't appear in week 2. They appear in month 6, year 2.

## Conclusion

If you're just starting and think you don't have enough talent: that's not the problem. The problem is showing up tomorrow, and the day after, and the day after that.

Talent is what people on the outside see after you've already done 1000 hours of work.
