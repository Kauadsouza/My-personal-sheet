## The question everyone asks me

"Why university? You already work, you have projects, you've made money with code. What good is a degree?"

It's a fair question. And my answer isn't simple.

## It's not about the diploma

Nobody hires a software engineer because of the paper they hold. They hire based on what they can do, what they've built, how they think.

I know that. But there's something a top university offers that YouTube tutorials don't: **the environment**.

## Why environment matters

At universities like MIT, Harvard, or Oxford, you're not surrounded by people who are good at programming. You're surrounded by people who solve problems that don't exist yet.

People who will found the next companies that change the world. People who will publish research that redefines entire fields.

That environment changes how you think about what's possible.

## The difference between knowing and understanding

I know how to code. I learned on YouTube, through real projects, by making a lot of mistakes.

But there are things I want to **understand**: algorithms in depth, distributed systems architecture, computation theory, AI from a mathematical perspective.

I can study these things on my own. But studying in the same place as the people who created these fields is different.

## The path I'm building

I'm not waiting for an acceptance to start. I'm building right now the portfolio of achievements that will make that application inevitable.

Real projects. Measurable impact. A story worth telling.

Ivy League or not, I want to be the kind of developer who doesn't need an institution to prove their worth. But it would be interesting to have it as a starting point for the next chapter.
